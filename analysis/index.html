Analysis

Our research investigates the intricate dynamics of Airbnb’s impact on the socioeconomic landscapes of London and New York City. We aim to uncover unique connections between Airbnb’s presence and the broader socio-economic fabric of these cities, exploring differences in their relationships with the global platform. Additionally, we focus on understanding changes in short-term rental supply and demand in response to regulatory adjustments, exploring the interplay between regulations, economic dynamics, and housing affordability and availability in both cities.

Licensing and Regulations

Figure 1 illustrates a pie chart showcasing the licensing status of individuals who reported their status in New York City.

Figure 1 provides insight into the situation, revealing that an alarming 92.42% of New York City owners operate without the required license for short-term rentals. This statistic prompts an in-depth inquiry into root causes, transcending statistical representation and raising profound concerns about the efficacy of regulatory measures. It necessitates a comprehensive assessment of Airbnb’s role in potentially facilitating and benefiting from regulatory violations. This revelation underscores a concerning trend and demands a closer examination of Airbnb’s adherence to local laws, particularly in the context of New York City’s stringent regulations.

According to Jeffrey-Wilensky, Jaclyn, and Christopher Werth (2023), short-term rentals in New York City are mandated to obtain a license. The substantial percentage of unlicensed owners prompts critical questions about overall compliance within the short-term rental landscape. The inquiry revolves around the number of listings operating without the necessary license and potential false claims of exemptions. Understanding the scope of this issue is paramount for assessing the platform’s adherence to regulatory requirements. Moreover, it raises an important exploration of how Airbnb continues to actively promote and profit from these unlicensed listings, posing potential legal and ethical concerns for both hosts and the platform.

Unlicensed short-term rentals, if widespread, can contribute to a reduction in the long-term housing supply, leading to increased competition for available housing units and potentially driving up prices. The operation of short-term rentals without the required license can exacerbate issues related to gentrification and have far-reaching socio-economic implications (BenÃ­tez-Aurioles and Tussyadiah, 2021). The resulting rise in property values and living costs may alter the social fabric of communities, potentially shifting demographics and impacting local businesses that cater to long-term residents (Boon et al., 2023). Moreover, as neighborhoods become more attractive to transient populations, the availability of affordable housing options may decrease, making it challenging for local residents to secure housing in their own communities (Ferreri and Sanyal, 2018). Addressing these challenges requires a nuanced approach, involving policymakers, community leaders, and property owners to consider the broader implications and work towards more inclusive and sustainable urban development (HÃ¼bscher and Kallert, 2023).

The regulatory landscape in New York City underwent a significant shift with the introduction of Local Law 18 on March 5, 2023, which promptly prohibited 2,300 short-term rentals. However, by August 28, only 257 out of 3,250 applications had received approval, leaving approximately 23,000 listings still active in the market (Schaal, 2023). The haphazard enforcement of these new regulations raises concerns about the efficacy of implementation and the extent of compliance within the short-term rental landscape.

In contrast to New York City’s Local Law 18, London’s approach involves setting a numerical threshold, acknowledging the transient nature of short-term rentals. In January 2017, Airbnb introduced a 90-day limit on ‘entire home’ listings in the Greater London area, stipulating that a property can only be rented out for short stays, such as through Airbnb, for a maximum of 90 days in a given calendar year. Once this threshold is reached, the property is expected to revert to more traditional, longer-term rental arrangements.

The rationale behind the 90-day limit is multifaceted, acknowledging the transient nature of short-term rentals and recognizing the economic benefits they can bring to hosts and the local economy. However, it also seeks to address concerns related to the impact of short-term rentals on the availability of housing for local residents. By imposing a cap, the policy aims to strike a balance between encouraging a sharing economy and ensuring that housing resources remain accessible for those who live and work in the city on a more permanent basis.

Nevertheless, the absence of detailed licensing data for London poses a challenge in fully dissecting the impacts of the 90-day limit. The project emphasizes the significance of considering such regulatory nuances to comprehensively understand the socio-economic effects of Airbnb in different urban contexts. It underscores the need for transparent and accessible data to facilitate nuanced analyses and guide informed policy decisions in navigating the evolving dynamics of short-term rentals in major global cities. Without such data, it remains challenging to explore and draw definitive conclusions about the potential connections between regulatory frameworks, unlicensed short-term rentals, and socio-economic impacts in London.

Note: The absence of detailed licensing data for London makes it challenging to draw definitive conclusions about potential connections between regulatory frameworks, unlicensed short-term rentals, and socio-economic impacts in the city.

Affluent areas, often characterized by better access to cultural events, expanding interests, and greater financial resources, play a significant role in shaping the socioeconomic landscape of neighborhoods. The higher costs of Airbnb stays in these areas not only reflect economic realities but also contribute to the ongoing transformation of these neighborhoods.

This pricing data provides valuable insights for researchers studying gentrification. The correlation between Airbnb pricing, neighborhood affluence, and demand underscores the complex relationship between tourism, economic development, and the reshaping of urban spaces. It prompts further exploration into how these pricing dynamics impact local communities, housing affordability, and the overall social fabric of these neighborhoods.

Understanding the link between Airbnb pricing and gentrification can inform policymakers on the potential impacts of short-term rentals on housing markets. It emphasizes the need for thoughtful regulation to balance the positive aspects of tourism with the preservation of local communities. As cities grapple with the challenges posed by gentrification, these insights become crucial for developing policies that foster inclusive urban development.
